Hello fellow travellers! I present to you my travelogue of Prague, Czech Republic - a wonderful city with a rich history and vibrant culture. Let's dive into the details of my trip!

## Day 1 - Arrival in Prague, Prague Castle, St. Vitus Cathedral

It started with a 4:00am alarm for a 6:00am flight from London Stansted. While taking the cross-country express to Stansted, I was surprised to see a lot of people heading to the airport at such an early hour. And their energy, my gosh :').
While at the airport, I did see some very planes with cool designs - Norwegian airlines. Definitely a step up from Ryanair from the looks of it.
The flight was smooth, and I arrived in Prague around 9:00am local time.
One of the most surprising things when I arrived at the PRG airport was the number of Asians I saw as tourists. Seemed to be a popular spot for Asians in Europe to visit Prague for holidays - especially if I was to compare it with my previous trip in Athens.
Once I was cleared through immigration, I took the 59 bus to the metro station.
While on my in bus 59 - I noticed how clean the motorway, with super clear road markings. Why can't the UK motorways be like this? I would've passed by driving exam sooner if the roads were like this fr.
I took the 59 bus all the way to the last stop before switching to the metro line A direct to Nádraží Veleslavín station. The metro system in Prague is very efficient and easy to navigate. I bought a 90-minute ticket for 40 CZK (Czech Koruna), which is approximately £1.42 (Thank God I can pay by card everywhere here). I really find the conversion to be annoying, but here is a quick reference:
```
10  CZK = 0.36£
100 CZK = 3.56£
```
The metro trains were the highlight of my journey. They were clean, modern, and had a unique design with blue lights on the floor that illuminated when the train was about to arrive. The metro lines are color-coded, making it easy to navigate even for first-time visitors. This is exactly like my favourite game Deus Ex : Mankind Divided, where the metro lines had a similar vibe. I attached a pic below - so you can see the similarity!

Apparently, the metro system in Prague is based on the Moscow metro system, which means no tapping is required when entering or exiting the metro. This system is based on trust, which is quite different from what I'm used to in London. However, ticket inspectors do check for tickets randomly, so it's essential to have a valid ticket while using the metro.

Anyhow getting off the metro station, I was greeted by a view of the <FORGOT> and a little water fountain. It was a bit chilly, but also getting sunny. After which I walked towards the Prague Castle entrance getting slightly lost in the way. The walk to the castle entrance was close - but the stairs. They were like a thousands of steps to reach the entrance. But once you reach the top - you get the view of the city. To be honest with you, it was not that impressive - and others there seemed to agree with me (I have overhead some tourists saying the same thing). I guess some were expecting too much from the view.

While also going up the stairs and later on too - I noticed a few Czech toys (teddys) and jewellery shops. I think it's a cultural thing here? No idea. Anyhow we're not here for that - we're here for the castle.

So the castle tickets have two tiers for multi-entry: The main circuit and the extended circuit. The main circuit includes entry to the main buildings like St. Vitus Cathedral, Old Royal Palace, St. George's Basilica, and Golden Lane. The extended circuit includes additional buildings like the Picture Gallery and the Toy Museum. I opted for the main circuit ticket, cause I had my expectations low for the castle.
I started first with the Old Royal castle itself - which was pretty mid. The main hall had been renovated quite a few times - the most recent being after the 2nd world war. The architecture was a mix of Gothic and Renaissance styles, which was interesting to see. However, I did find it a bit underwhelming compared to other castles I've visited ever.
Some of their rooms also had these crest painting designs on the walls - which I found quite interesting. Apparently, these designs were used to represent different noble families in the past.
After exploring the main castle, I headed to St. Vitus Cathedral, which was this massive Gothic architecture cathedral. The cathedral is a stunning example of Gothic architecture, with intricate details and beautiful stained glass windows, and a massive organ. The interior was breathtaking, with high vaulted ceilings and ornate decorations. I spent quite some time admiring the stained glass paintings and taking in the serene atmosphere.
The rest of the main circuit was pretty boring - St. George's Basilica, which was very meh and the Golden Lane, which was a row of small colourful houses that were once home to castle guards and goldsmiths. The houses have been converted into shops and museums, showcasing the history of the area.
However, there was this one spot which caught my attention - the Daliborka Tower. This tower was used as a prison in the past, and it had a dark history. The tower was named after Dalibor of Kozojedy, a knight who was imprisoned there in the 15th century. The tower has a unique design, with a spiral staircase that leads to the cellar. The cell was cramped and dark, with a small window that provided little light. It was a sobering reminder of the harsh conditions prisoners faced in the past. There is also this hole on the floor of the tower, which was used to lower prisoners into the cellar. The hole is now covered with a glass panel, allowing visitors to see the dark and damp conditions below.
It looked pretty grim in my opinion. I couldn't imagine being locked up in such a place for even a hour!

After all this I decided to take a walk to the nearby Queen Anne's Summer Palace, which is a beautiful Renaissance building located within the castle complex. The palace was built in the 16th century as a summer residence for Queen Anne of Bohemia, and it features stunning architecture and beautiful gardens. You couldn't enter in - but the gardens were nice to walk around in.

After all this I decided to grab some grub before I walk to the next highlight of the day - The Franz Kafka Museum.
